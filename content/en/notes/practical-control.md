_“You are in control” is one of the most common promises in digital systems._

It can mean that a person chooses what to share, confirms a request, manages a credential, changes a setting, controls a profile, or decides whether to use a service. The promise is attractive because it suggests autonomy: the system does not simply act on people; it lets them act for themselves.

That promise can matter. A system that gives people real choices may be better than one that hides decisions from them or treats them only as passive subjects.

But control in a system description is not always the same as control in real life.

Think of a person asked to approve a request on a screen. The button is there. The choice is formally theirs. But the meaning of the request may be unclear, the consequences may be hard to judge, and refusal may close the only practical route to the service they need. In that situation, the presence of a choice does not by itself tell us how much control the person really has.

This is the gap that practical control tries to name.

Practical control is not only about whether a button, dashboard, consent screen, or setting exists. It asks whether people can actually understand, use, refuse, correct, recover, or get help within the conditions they face.

A person may be given settings without having a realistic way to manage them. They may be told they have alternatives while one route is much easier or more necessary than the others. They may be treated as responsible for an error even when the system was too complex to navigate safely.

These questions become especially important when digital systems are used to access services, prove identity, manage credentials, receive benefits, participate in a process, or interact with public institutions. In those settings, “user control” is not just a design feature. It can become part of the justification for the system.

A system can promise control and still make control difficult to exercise.  
It can offer choices and still make one route practically unavoidable.  
It can assign responsibility to the user while leaving the user with little real power to understand or manage the risk.

This does not mean that user control is meaningless. Formal controls can be valuable. Consent screens, dashboards, settings, logs, warnings, and approval steps can give people important forms of agency. They can make systems less hidden and less one-sided.

But their value depends on the surrounding conditions. A control is stronger when it is understandable, reachable, reversible where appropriate, supported when needed, and connected to usable routes for [correction or complaint]({{< relref "what-makes-a-system-contestable.md" >}}). It is weaker when it mainly transfers work, risk, or blame to the person using the system.

This is why practical control is a public question, not only a design question.

Design may ask whether the interface gives people options. Law and policy may ask whether consent, authorisation, or responsibility has been formally assigned. Public administration may ask whether people can complete the process and receive support. Infrastructure studies may ask what kind of “normal user” the system assumes. Political theory may ask whether people remain meaningfully able to act, refuse, correct, or challenge within arrangements that affect their public standing.

The point is not to reject control language. It is to make it concrete.

A useful question is therefore:

When a system says that people are in control, what must be true for that control to be real?

The answer will not be the same in every case. But it should include more than a formal choice. Practical control depends on understanding, realistic alternatives, support, recovery, correction, and a fair allocation of [responsibility]({{< relref "responsibility-does-not-disappear-when-systems-are-distributed.md" >}}).

Without those conditions, control can become a promise that is formally present but too weak to carry the public weight placed on it.
