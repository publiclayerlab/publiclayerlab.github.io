Interoperability is often presented as a practical good: systems should work together.

A person should not have to carry the same proof from one office to another. A public body should not need to rebuild information it already has. A record, a credential — a document, certificate, or digital proof that another system is expected to trust — or a decision should be able to travel where it is needed.

That promise is real. Fragmented systems can make public life harder than it needs to be. They can force people to repeat information, chase documents, explain themselves again, or act as messengers between institutions that should be able to coordinate. In practice, this can increase the [burden]({{< relref "burden-proportionality.md" >}}) placed on the person using the system.

But systems do not work together by magic.

Before information can move, something has to be made common. A format has to be readable. A category has to mean enough of the same thing in more than one place. An identifier has to point to the right person, organisation, object, or record. A credential has to be trusted outside the place where it was issued.

This is why interoperability is not only a matter of connection. It is also a matter of settlement.

Settlement here means the shared terms that make connection possible: categories, identifiers, formats, permissions, and assumptions that may become difficult to see once the system is working.

To make systems work together, institutions often need shared standards, protocols, vocabularies, identifiers, rules, and expectations. They need to decide what counts as the same person, the same address, the same organisation, the same status, or the same kind of proof. They also need to decide what happens when those things do not match.

That can make public systems easier to use. Interoperability can reduce duplication, make services more coherent, and prevent people from carrying the burden of institutional fragmentation. It can help information travel with less friction and help institutions act on records or credentials they can trust.

But the same qualities that make interoperability useful can also make it consequential.

When systems work together, an error may travel further. A category used in one setting may become important in another. A credential accepted by one institution may become a condition for access elsewhere.

Even a small mismatch can become difficult to place. One register may spell a name differently from another. One system may treat an address, status, or identifier as settled while another does not. The person affected may not know which record is wrong, which institution can correct it, or why one system’s version has become decisive.

A system can be interoperable and still be hard to question. It can reduce paperwork for many people and still create new difficulties for those whose records do not match. It can improve coordination while making [responsibility]({{< relref "responsibility-does-not-disappear-when-systems-are-distributed.md" >}}) harder to locate.

That is why interoperability should be treated as a public design question, not only as an engineering goal.

Law and policy ask what information may be shared, who may rely on it, and what safeguards apply. Public administration sees the service routes and institutional responsibilities that interoperability is meant to simplify. Infrastructure studies notice the standards, identifiers, data models, and classifications that make systems able to recognise one another. Political theory asks whether the resulting arrangements remain justified, accountable, and open to challenge.

The public issue is not whether systems should ever connect. Many systems should. The issue is what kind of connection is being built, on whose terms, with what safeguards, and with what routes for correction when the match fails.

A useful question is therefore:

When systems are made to work together, who decides what has to be made common — and who carries the burden when the match fails?

Interoperability is valuable because public systems should not force people to navigate unnecessary fragmentation. But connection is never only connection. It also decides what can travel, what must fit, and what kind of responsibility follows information as it moves.
