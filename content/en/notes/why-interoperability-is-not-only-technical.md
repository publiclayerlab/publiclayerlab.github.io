Interoperability often sounds like a technical ambition: connect the systems, align the standards, make information move.

That ambition can be valuable. Public systems that cannot work together can produce repetition, delay, errors, and unnecessary burden. Technical standards, shared formats, and reliable connections can make public services easier to coordinate and easier to use.

But connection is not the whole problem. Systems may be able to exchange information and still disagree about what that information means.

One system may treat a status as temporary while another treats it as final. One office may accept a form of proof that another does not. One register may hold a record that another system uses but cannot [correct]({{< relref "repair-is-part-of-public-system-design.md" >}}). One portal may show a result while responsibility for the underlying information sits somewhere else.

This is why interoperability sits between technical design, law, administration, and public authority. Technically, the question may be whether systems can exchange data. Legally, it may be what information may be shared and who is responsible if it is wrong. Administratively, it may be who can correct a record or resolve a mismatch. Publicly, it may be whether the person affected can understand what happened and challenge the result.

Shared categories can make coordination easier. They can also decide what fits and what does not. A common identifier, status, permission, or evidence rule may look like a background detail once the system is working. But it can shape whether a person is recognised, which route they are sent through, which proof is accepted, and who must repair the problem when the systems do not [line up]({{< relref "why-hand-offs-matter.md" >}}).

Not every technical standard carries the same public weight. The issue carries more public weight when interoperability affects access, recognition, correction, responsibility, or the burdens placed on people.

The useful question is not only whether systems can connect. It is what their connection makes common, who is responsible when that common ground fails, and whether people can still correct what follows from it.
