If a system is lawful, authorised, and compliant, it may seem as if the main public question has been answered.

Often, those conditions are necessary. A public system that ignores legal authority, procedural safeguards, or rights protections cannot rescue itself by saying that it is efficient, innovative, or useful. Compliance is not a minor technicality. It is one of the ways public power is constrained.

But legality does not settle every public question. A system can follow the rules and still be difficult to understand. A process can meet formal requirements while making correction almost impossible in practice. A complaint route can exist on paper while remaining hard to find, hard to use, or disconnected from the part of the system that caused the problem.

Not every poor interface or frustrating procedure becomes a legitimacy problem. Some complexity is unavoidable, and public institutions cannot remove every burden from every process. The question becomes sharper when a system helps organise access to rights, services, public records, participation, protection, or official recognition. In those cases, the difference between a formal route and a usable route can matter politically.

People often encounter public authority not only through laws, offices, or official decisions, but through portals, forms, categories, eligibility checks, proof requirements, notifications, registers, automated routes, and help desks. These arrangements can shape what people are asked to prove, which route they are sent through, how errors are detected, and where responsibility appears to sit.

At that point, the question is not only whether a rule has been followed. It is also whether the arrangement can carry the public weight placed on it. Can affected people understand what has happened? Can they correct a mistake? Are they being asked to carry too much proof, navigation, or repair work themselves?

None of this makes compliance less important. It makes the public question wider. A lawful system may still need better explanation, clearer responsibility, fairer [burden distribution]({{< relref "burden-proportionality.md" >}}), or a more usable route for [correction and challenge]({{< relref "what-makes-a-system-contestable.md" >}}).

Legal compliance is a necessary public discipline. It is not always the end of public responsibility.
