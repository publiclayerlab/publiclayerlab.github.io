Sometimes the system works only because the person does the work of making it work.

They find the right form, interpret the instruction, gather the proof, repeat information already held somewhere else, wait for a response, correct an error they did not create, and try to discover which office, portal, or procedure is responsible. None of these burdens may look dramatic on its own. Together, they can decide whether a public route is actually usable.

A burden is not unfair simply because it exists. Public systems often need evidence, records, checks, appointments, signatures, updates, or waiting periods. Some friction protects people against mistakes, fraud, arbitrariness, or unequal treatment. A system without any procedure would not automatically be fairer.

The question is whether the burden is proportionate. Is it connected to a clear public purpose? Is it explained in a way people can act on? Is it heavier than necessary? Is it placed where it can reasonably be carried?

This matters because burdens are not distributed evenly. A form that is merely annoying for one person may be decisive for someone with limited time, language access, money, health, confidence, digital tools, or institutional knowledge. A correction route that looks available on paper may still be too difficult to use when the person affected is already under pressure.

Burden can also be hidden inside the design of a system. A portal may make submission faster while making correction harder. A standard category may improve consistency while leaving unusual situations without a clear route. A dashboard may show a status without showing who can change it. A digital process may reduce paperwork for some people while making others responsible for repairing mismatched records.

The issue is not digital versus non-digital. Digital systems can reduce burden, simplify procedures, and make public routes easier to use. But they can also move work out of sight. They can make the person affected responsible for discovering the problem, translating the requirement, proving the mismatch, and [carrying information between institutions]({{< relref "why-hand-offs-matter.md" >}}) that do not coordinate well.

A public system does not become fair simply because a route exists. The route also has to be one that people can [realistically use]({{< relref "practical-control.md" >}}).

Burden proportionality asks a simple public question: who is being made to do the work, and is that a fair place to put it?
