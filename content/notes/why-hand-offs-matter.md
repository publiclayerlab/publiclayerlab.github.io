A hand-off can look like a small administrative step: one form goes to another office, one record moves to another system, one question is passed to another team.

Hand-offs are not automatically a problem. Public systems need specialist roles, shared records, review routes, technical systems, and cooperation between institutions. The issue begins when the case moves but the context does not.

A hand-off is not only a transfer of information. It is also a test of whether context, responsibility, and correction routes travel with the case. A record may move without the reason behind it. A question may be passed on without the authority to answer it. A correction request may arrive without the history needed to understand it.

This matters because [repair]({{< relref "repair-is-part-of-public-system-design.md" >}}) often fails at the hand-off. A person may be told to contact another office, but the second office may not have the relevant information. A portal may display a status created somewhere else, but not show who can change it. A record may be reused by another system while the route for correcting it becomes harder to find.

Digital systems can improve hand-offs. They can preserve context, show ownership, log changes, reduce repetition, and make correction routes clearer. But they can also move information quickly without moving [responsibility]({{< relref "responsibility-does-not-disappear-when-systems-are-distributed.md" >}}) clearly. A case can travel faster than the explanation, authority, or repair route needed to make sense of it.

When authority does not travel with the case, the person affected may be left repeating information without reaching anyone who can act. One office may receive the question but not the record. Another may see the record but not the reason it is being challenged. A portal may show that something has moved, while the correction route remains attached to the place it came from. The hand-off has happened, but the capacity to understand or repair the issue has not moved with it.

This becomes more than an internal process issue when a hand-off affects access to rights, services, public records, official recognition, protection, or necessary support.

A hand-off works when more than the case moves. The receiving part also needs enough context, responsibility, and authority to explain, correct, or escalate what has been passed to it.
