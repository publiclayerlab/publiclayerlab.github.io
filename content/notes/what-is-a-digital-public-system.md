A digital public system is not just a website or an app.

It is a digital or administrative arrangement through which people meet institutions. It may be a municipal portal, a benefits form, an identity check, a public register, a participation platform, a cooperative governance tool, a complaints route, or an AI-assisted process that helps sort or assess information.

The visible screen is only one part of the system. Behind it there may be rules, forms, data fields, eligibility categories, records, standards, workflows, hand-offs, logs, review routes, and organisational routines. Together, these parts shape what people can do and what institutions can see.

That is why a digital public system can matter even when it does not make the final decision. A form can decide which situation fits. A register can decide what becomes visible. An identity check can decide who enters a process. A workflow can decide when a case reaches a human reviewer. A dashboard or AI-assisted summary can influence which signals are noticed and which ones disappear.

Public Layer Lab uses the term to keep these arrangements in view together, while recognising that the stichting’s wider statutory scope also includes digital and administrative systems of socially relevant institutions. The point is not to say that every digital tool is a public authority. The point is simpler: when digital and administrative systems shape access, recognition, decisions, explanation, or repair, they become part of how public and civic life is organised.

A useful question is therefore not only:

> Does the system work?

It is also:

> What does the system make visible, what does it make difficult, and how can people understand or challenge what happens?
